In Table 2.6.2, the ten most common rules of inference are listed. Note that all of these are equivalent to tautologies that involve conditionals (as opposed to biconditionals), every one of the basic logical equivalences that we established in Section 2.3 is really a tautology involving a biconditional, collectively these are known as the “rules of replacement.” In an argument, any statement allows us to infer a logically equivalent statement. Or, put differently, we could replace any premise with a different, but logically equivalent, premise. You might enjoy trying to determine a minimal set of rules of inference, that together with the rules of replacement would allow one to form all of the same arguments as the ten rules in Table 2.6.2.
