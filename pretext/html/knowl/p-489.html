Deduction is the process by which we determine new truths from old. It is sometimes claimed that nothing truly new can come from deduction, the truth of a statement that is arrived at by deductive processes was lying (perhaps hidden somewhat) within the hypotheses. This claim is something of a canard, as any Sherlock Holmes aficionado can tell you, the statements that can sometimes be deduced from others can be remarkably surprising. A better argument against deduction is that it is a relatively ineffective way for most human beings to discover new truths â€” for that purpose inductive processes are superior for the majority of us. Nevertheless, if a chain of deductive reasoning leading from known hypotheses to a particular conclusion can be exhibited, the truth of the conclusion is unassailable. For this reason, mathematicians have latched on to deductive reasoning as the tool for, if not discovering our theorems, communicating them to others.
