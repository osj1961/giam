Proof

We count the total of the entries in the magic square in two ways. The sum of all the entries in the magic square is \begin{equation*} S = 1 + 2 + 3 + \ldots + n^2. \end{equation*}

Using the formula for the sum of the first \(k\) naturals ( \(\sum_{i=1}^k i = \frac{k^2+k}{2}\)) and evaluating at \(n^2\) gives \begin{equation*} S = \frac{n^4 + n^2}{2}. \end{equation*}

On the other hand, if the magic sum is \(M\), then each of the \(n\) rows has numbers in it which sum to \(M\) so \begin{equation*} S = nM. \end{equation*}

By equating these different expressions for \(S\) and solving for \(M\), we prove the desired result: \begin{equation*} nM = \frac{n^4 + n^2}{2}, \end{equation*} therefore \begin{equation*} M = \frac{n^3 + n}{2}. \end{equation*}
