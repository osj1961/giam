Proof

(By strong induction) Consider an arbitrary natural number \(n>1\). If \(n\) is prime then \(n\) clearly has a prime factor (itself), so suppose that \(n\) is not prime. By definition, a composite natural number can be factored, so \(n=a \cdot b\) for some pair of natural numbers \(a\) and \(b\) which are both greater than 1. Since \(a\) and \(b\) are factors of \(n\) both greater than 1, it follows that \(a \lt n\) (it is also true that \(b \lt n\) but we don't need that â€¦). The inductive hypothesis can now be applied to deduce that \(a\) has a prime factor \(p\). Since \(p \divides a\) and \(a \divides n\), by transitivity \(p \divides n\). Thus \(n\) has a prime factor.
