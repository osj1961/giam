Another perfectly lovely argument involving parity arises in questions concerning whether or not it is possible to tile a pruned chessboard with dominoes. We've seen dominoes before in Section 5.1 and we're just hoping you've run across chessboards before. Usually a chessboard is 8 × 8, but we would like to adopt a more liberal interpretation that a chessboard can be any rectangular grid of squares we might choose. 3 The game known as “draughts” in the UK and “checkers” in the US is played on an \(8 \times 8\) board, but (for example) international draughts is played on a \(10 \times 10\) board and Canadian checkers is played on a \(12 \times 12\) board. Suppose that we have a supply of dominoes that are of just the right size that if they are laid on a chessboard they perfectly cover two adjacent squares. Our first question is quite simple. Is it possible to perfectly tile an \(m \times n\) chessboard with such dominoes?
