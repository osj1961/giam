Proof

We proceed by induction on \(n\).

Basis: Notice that when \(n=0\) the sum on the left-hand side has no terms in it! This is known as an empty sum, and by definition, an empty sum's value is \(0\). Also, when \(n=0\) the formula on the right-hand side becomes \((0 \cdot 1)/2\) and this is \(0\) as well. 1 If you'd prefer to avoid the “empty sum” argument, you can choose to use \(n=1\) as the basis case. The theorem should be restated so the universe of discourse is positive naturals.

Inductive step: Consider the sum on the left-hand side of the \(k+1\)–th version of our formula. \begin{equation*} \sum_{j=1}^{k+1} j \end{equation*}

We can separate out the last term of this sum. \begin{equation*} = (k+1) + \sum_{j=1}^{k} j \end{equation*}

Next, we can use the inductive hypothesis to replace the sum (the part that goes from 1 to \(k\)) with a formula. \begin{equation*} = (k+1) + \frac{k(k+1)}{2} \end{equation*}

From here on out it's just algebra … \begin{equation*} = \frac{2(k+1)}{2} + \frac{k(k+1)}{2} \end{equation*} \begin{equation*} = \frac{2(k+1) + k(k+1)}{2} \end{equation*} \begin{equation*} = \frac{(k+1) \cdot (k+2)}{2}. \end{equation*}
