Now, every element of the disjoint union of \(A\) and \(B\) is in one of these sequences. Also, it is easy to see that these sequences are either disjoint or identical. Taking these two facts together it follows that these sequences form a partition of \(A \cup B\). We'll define a bijection \(\phi : A \longrightarrow B\) by deciding what it must do on these sequences. There are four possibilities for how the sequences we've just defined can play out. In extending them to the left, we may run into a place where one of the inverse functions needed isn't defined â€” or not. Similarly, we can define a \(B\)-stopper. If the inverse functions are always defined within a given sequence there are also two possibilities; the sequence may be finite (and so it must be cyclic in nature) or the sequence may be truly infinite.
